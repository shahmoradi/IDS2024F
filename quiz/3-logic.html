Quiz 3: Inference and Logic Fundamentals

by Amir Shahmoradi — 16 Oct 2024

This quiz aims at testing your basic knowledge of Scientific Inference and Logic.
Don’t forget to push your answers to your remote repository by the end of quiz time.
Push all your answers to quiz/3/ folder in your Github project.

Hint.

  1. Remember that in mathematics and Data Science, especially when proving or deriving something (like drawing Venn diagrams or Truth Tables) it is best to split the whole process (solution) to the tiniest steps possible and achieve them one after the other.
  2. All derivation and proof questions are based on what we have covered in lecture slides and HW.


  1. Name and describe briefly the steps involved in scientific inference.

    Answer:

    1. Observational data collection and reduction.
    2. Hypothesis formation.
    3. Calibration.
    4. Validation.
    5. Prediction.

  2. Name the three pillars of science.

    Answer:
    Theory, Experiment, Computation.

  3. Describe three different major applications of the third pillar of science with respect to the first two.
    (Hint: Recall the discussions we had early in the semester during the first lecture series. Where and how could the third pillar replace the other pillars?)

    Answer:

    1. Simulations can serve as theory where not available.
    2. Simulations can serve as experimental data where not available.
    3. Simulations can be used to predict quantities that are not readily or currently measurable.

  4. Name the two different categories of logical reasoning and provide and example of each class.

    Answer:

    1. Deductive reasoning: If A is true, then B is true.
    2. Plausible reasoning: If A is true, then B is more likely to be true.

  5. What is the relationship between data and reality? Can we observe reality?
    (Hint: Recall the discussions we had early in the semester during the first lecture series. Remember the pyramid of scientific inference.)

    Answer:
    Data is what we observe. Reality is what we never observed because it is always obfuscated with noise.

  6. What is the difference between model validation and calibration?

    Answer:
    Calibration is about finding the best-fit parameters of the model and validation is about validating the predictions of the model on a test dataset.

  7. Is logical implication the same as physical causation? Explain with an example.

    Answer:
    No. “The sky is rainy” implies that “the sky is cloudy”. However, rainy sky did not physically cause the cloudy sky.

  8. Who is the Boolean algebra named after?

    Answer:
    George Boole

  11. What does it mean if two Boolean propositions are equal?

    Answer:
    It means they have the same truth value.

  12. What is the result of adding two propositions that complement each other?
    Explain you answer with an example.

    Answer:
    Proposition A: The sky is sunny.
    Proposition B: The sky is NOT sunny.
    A and B are complementary. Therefore, the result of \(A + B\) is always true, because the sky is always either sunny or not sunny at any given time.

  13. What is the result of multiplying (that is, conjunction of) two propositions that complement each other?
    Explain you answer with an example.

    Answer:
    Proposition A: The sky is sunny.
    Proposition B: The sky is NOT sunny.
    A and B are complementary. Therefore, the result of \(AB\) is always true, because the sky can never be both sunny and not sunny at the same time.

  14. Display the logical implication $A \Rightarrow B$ via a Venn diagram.
    (Hint. Think about the meaning of logical implication $A \Rightarrow B$: If $A$ is true, then $B$ must be true. If $A$ is false, then $B$ can be either true or false. What configuration of $A$ and $B$ circles in a Venn diagram would correspond to the above statement for logical implication? Recall that when you are inside an object (circle) in a Venn diagram, it means the proposition corresponding to that object is true. When you are outside, the proposition is false.)

    Answer:
    See the the lecture notes.

  15. What is the tiniest unit of information?

    Answer:
    A bit.

  16. Imagine our universe was completely empty with nothing in it. Does this universe contain any information?

    Answer:
    No. To have the tiniest bit of information, we have to have at least two contrasting entities, like true vs. false, or 0 vs. 1, or something against nothing.

  17. Consider 4 bits of storage. How many integers (or call it different states, or different possibilities) can you represent with it?

    Answer:
    \(2^4\).

  18. Consider 4 boxes each of which can take 3 possible values (or call it states): \(-1, 0, +1\). How many decimal integers (or call it different states, or different possibilities) can you represent with these 4 boxes?

    Answer:
    \(3^4\).
